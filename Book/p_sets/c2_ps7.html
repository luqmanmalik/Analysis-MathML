07. Use the method of problem six to show that $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n k^p$ can always be written in the form

$\dfrac{n^{p+1}}{p+1}+An^p+Bn^{p-1}+Cn^{p-2}+\dots$

The first ten such expressions are

$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k=\frac{1}{2}n^2+\frac{1}{2}n$
$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k^2=\frac{1}{3}n^3+\frac{1}{2}n^2+\frac{1}{6}n$
$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k^3=\frac{1}{4}n^4+\frac{1}{2}n^3+\frac{1}{4}n^2$
$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k^4=\frac{1}{5}n^5+\frac{1}{2}n^4+\frac{1}{3}n^3-\frac{1}{30}n$
$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k^5=\frac{1}{6}n^6+\frac{1}{2}n^5+\frac{5}{12}n^4-\frac{1}{12}n^2$
$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k^6=\frac{1}{7}n^7+\frac{1}{2}n^6+\frac{1}{2}n^5-\frac{1}{6}n^3+\frac{1}{42}n$

$\dots$ $\displaystyle\sum_{k=i}^n k^7 = \frac{1}{8}n^8+\frac{1}{2}n^7+\frac{7}{12}n^6-\frac{7}{24}n^4+\frac{1}{12}n^2$ $\displaystyle\sum_{k=i}^n k^8 = \frac{1}{9}n^9+\frac{1}{2}n^8+\frac{2}{3}n^7-\frac{7}{15}n^5+\frac{2}{9}n^3-\frac{1}{30}n$ $\displaystyle\sum_{k=i}^n k^9 = \frac{1}{10}n^{10}+\frac{1}{2}n^9+\frac{3}{4}n^8-\frac{7}{10}n^6+\frac{1}{4}n^4-\frac{3}{20}n^2$

$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^n k^{10}=\frac{1}{11}n^{11}+\frac{1}{2}n^{10}+\frac{5}{6}n^9-1n^7+1n^5-\frac{1}{2}n^3+\frac{5}{66}n.$

Notice that the coefficients in the second column are always $\frac{1}{2},$ and that after the third column the powers of $n$ with nonzero coefficients decrease by two until $n^2$ or $n$ is reached. The coefficients in all but the first two columns seem to be rather haphazard, but there actually is some sort of pattern; finding it may be regarded as a super–perspicacity test. See Problem 27–17 for the complete story.
