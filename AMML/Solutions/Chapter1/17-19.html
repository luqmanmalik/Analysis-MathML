(a) We may find this value directly by completing the square. (Notice that there is a good amount of reverse engineering occurring in 17(c) in particular.) Since

$ \begin{align} \displaystyle 2x^2-3x+4 &= 2x^2-3x+\frac{9}{8}+4-\frac{9}{8} \\ &= 2\bigg(x^2-\frac{6x}{4}+\frac{9}{8}\bigg)+4-\frac{9}{8} \\ &= 2\bigg(x^2-\frac{3x}{4}-\frac{3x}{4}+\frac{9}{16}\bigg)+4-\frac{9}{8} \\ &= 2 \bigg(x-\frac{3}{4}\bigg)^2+4-\frac{9}{8} \\ &= 2 \bigg(x-\frac{3}{4}\bigg)^2 +\frac{23}{8}, \end{align} $

the smallest possible value is 23/8, when $(x-3/4)=0,$ or $x=3/4. \ \ \square$
(b) Similarly, we have

$ \begin{align} \displaystyle x^2-3x+2y^2+4y+2 &= x^2-3x+2y^2+4y+2+\bigg(\frac{9}{4}-\frac{9}{4}\bigg) \\ &= x^2-3x+\frac{9}{4}+2y^2+4y+2-\frac{9}{4} \\ &= \bigg(x-\frac{3}{2}\bigg)\bigg(x-\frac{3}{2}\bigg)+2(y^2+2y+1)-\frac{9}{4} \\ &= \bigg(x-\frac{3}{2}\bigg)^2+2(y+1)^2-\frac{9}{4}, \\ \end{align} $

so the smallest possible value is $-9/4,$ when $x=3/2$ and $y=1. \ \ \square$

$ \require{cancel} \begin{align} x^2+4xy+5y^2 &- 4x-6y+7 \\ &= x^2+4xy-4x+5y^2-6y+7 \\ &= x^2+4x(y-1)+5y^2-6y+7 \\ &= x^2+2[2x(y-1)]+5y^2-6y+7 \\ &= x^2+2x(y-1)+2x(y-1)+ \color{grey}{4(y-1)^2} - \color{grey}{4(y-1)^2} \\ & \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ +5y^2-6y+7 \\ &= [x+2(y-1)][x+2(y-1)]-4(y-1)^2 \\ & \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ +5y^2-6y+7 \\ &= [x+2(y-1)]^2+5y^2-6y+7-4(y-1)^2 \\ &= [x+2(y-1)]^2+5y^2-6y+7-4(y^2-2y+1) \\ &= [x+2(y-1)]^2+5y^2-6y+7-4y^2+8y-4) \\ &= [x+2(y-1)]^2+y^2+2y+3 \\ &= [x+2(y-1)]^2+(y+1)^2+2, \\ \end{align} $

18.
(a) To show that the numbers under consideration satisfy the given equation, we will prove the quadratic equation by completing the square. First, note that $x^2+bx+c=0 \ \Leftrightarrow \ ax^2+bx+c=0,$ where $x=1$. It then follows that $$ \begin{equation} \begin{split} x^2+\frac{bx}{a}+\frac{c}{a} &= \frac{0}{a} \\ x^2+\frac{bx}{a}+\frac{c}{a} &= 0 \\ x^2+\frac{bx}{a}+\frac{c}{a}-\bigg(\frac{c}{a}\bigg) &= 0-\bigg(\frac{c}{a}\bigg) \\ x^2+\frac{bx}{a} &= -\frac{c}{a} \\ x^2+\frac{bx}{a}+\bigg(\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)^2 &= -\frac{c}{a}+\bigg(\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)^2 \\ \bigg(x+\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)^2 &= -\frac{c}{a}+\frac{b^2}{4a^2} \\ \bigg(x+\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)^2 &= -\frac{4ac}{4a^2}+\frac{b^2}{4a^2} \\ \bigg(x+\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)^2 &= \frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2} \\ \sqrt{\bigg(x+\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)^2} &= \sqrt{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}} \\ x+\frac{b}{2a} &= \pm\sqrt{\frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}} \\ x+\frac{b}{2a} &= \pm\frac{\displaystyle\sqrt{{b}^2-4ac}}{2a} \\ x+\frac{b}{2a}-\bigg(\frac{b}{2a}\bigg) &= -\bigg(\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)\pm\frac{\displaystyle\sqrt{{b}^2-4ac}}{2a} \\ x &= -\bigg(\frac{b}{2a}\bigg)\pm\frac{\displaystyle\sqrt{{b}^2-4ac}}{2a} \\ x &= \frac{-b\pm\displaystyle\sqrt{{b}^2-4ac}}{2a}. \end{split} \end{equation} $$ So, clearly the desired numbers satisfy the given equation, particular in the case $a=1. \ \ \square$
(b) We may capture this result by forcing the following contradiction.

$ \begin{align} x^2+bx+c &= x^2+bx+\frac{b^2}{4}+c-\frac{b^2}{4} \\ &= \bigg(x+\frac{b}{2}\bigg)^2+\bigg(c-\frac{b^2}{4}\bigg) \\ &\geq c-\frac{b^2}{4}; \end{align} $

but $\displaystyle (b^2-4c < 0) \ = \ (-4c < -b^2) \ = \ (c > b^2/4) \ = \ [0 < c-(b^2/4)].$ which contradicts our original assumption, so $x^2+bx+c>0$ for all $x. \ \ \square$
(c) Applying part (b) with $y$ for $b$ and $y^2$ for $c:$ we have $b^2-4c=y^2-4y^2 < 0$ for $y \neq 0,$ so $x^2+xy+y^2>0$ for all $x$ if $y \neq 0$ (and surely $x^2+xy+y^2>0$ for all $x \neq 0$ if $y=0$). $ \ \ \square$
(d) $\alpha$ must satisfy $(\alpha y)^2-4y^2 < 0$ or $\alpha ^2 < 4$ or $| \alpha | < 2$. $ \ \ \square$
(e) Since

$\displaystyle x^2+bx+c=\bigg(x+\frac{b}{2}\bigg)^2+\bigg(c-\frac{b^2}{4}\bigg) \geq c-\frac{b^2}{4},$

and since $x^2+bx+c$ has the value $c-b^2/4$ when $x=-b/2,$ the minimum value is $c-b^2/4.$ Also, since

$\displaystyle ax^2+bx+c=a\bigg(x^2+\frac{bx}{a}+\frac{c}{a}\bigg),$

the minimum value is

$\displaystyle a\bigg(\frac{c}{a}-\frac{b^2}{4a^2}\bigg)=c-\frac{b^2}{4a}. \ \ \square$
