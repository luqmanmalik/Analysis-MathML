03. If $0 \leq k \leq n,$ the binomial coefficient $\displaystyle {n \choose k}$ is defined by

$\displaystyle{n \choose k}=\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}=\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)}{k!}, \ \ \ \text{if} \ k\neq0,n$

$\displaystyle{n \choose 0}={n \choose n}=1$ (a special case of the first formula if we define $0!=1,)$ and for $k < 0$ or $n < k$ we just define the binomial coefficient to be 0.

(a) Prove that $$\displaystyle{n+1 \choose k}=\displaystyle{n \choose k-1}+\displaystyle{n \choose k}.$$ The proof does not require an induction argument.

This relation gives rise to the following configuration, known as "Pascal's triangle" — a number not on one of the sides is the sum of the two numbers above it; the binomial coefficient $\displaystyle{n \choose k}$ is the $(k+1)\text{st}$ number in the $(n+1)\text{st}$ row. $$ \begin{array} & & & & & & 1 \\ & & & & 1 & & 1 \\ & & & 1 & & 2 & & 1 \\ & & 1 & & 3 & & 3 & & 1 \\ & 1 & & 4 & & 6 & & 4 & & 1 \\ 1 & & 5 & & 10 & & 10 & & 5 & & 1 \\ & & & & & \dots \end{array} $$
(b) Notice that all the numbers in Pascal's triangle are natural numbers. Use part (a) to prove by induction that $\displaystyle{n \choose k}$ is always a natural number. (Your entire proof by induction will, in a sense, be summed up in a glance by Pascal's triangle.)
(c) Give another proof that $\displaystyle{n \choose k}$ is a natural number by showing that $\displaystyle{n \choose k}$ is the number of sets of exactly $k$ itegers each chosen from $1,\dots, n.$
(d) Prove the binomial theorem: If $a$ and $b$ are any numbers and $n$ is a natural number, then $$ \begin{equation} \begin{split} \displaystyle (a+b)^n &= a^n+{n \choose 1}a^{n-1}b+{n \choose 2}a^{n-2}b^2+\cdots+{n \choose n-1}ab^{n-1}+b^n \\ &= \sum_{j=0}^{n} {n \choose j}a^{n-j}b^j. \end{split} \end{equation} $$
(e) Prove that

$ \begin{array}{ll} \text{(i)} & \displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^{n}{n \choose j}={n \choose 0}+\cdots+{n \choose n}=2^n. \\ \text{(ii)} & \displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^{n}(-1)^j{n \choose j}={n \choose 0}-{n \choose 1}+\cdots\pm{n \choose n}=0. \\ \text{(iii)} & \displaystyle\sum_{l \ \text{odd}}{n \choose l}={n \choose 1}+{n \choose 3}+\cdots=2^{n-1}. \\ \text{(vi)} & \displaystyle\sum_{l \ \text{even}}{n \choose l}={n \choose 0}+{n \choose 2}+\cdots=2^{n-1}. \end{array} $
